For most USD-quoted forex pairs the pip value per standard lot is about $10. For JPY pairs, indices and futures it differs — check your instrument and adjust the field. This tool is for education, not advice.

The formula behind it

There's no magic here — just one line you should know by heart:

Lot size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Stop in pips × Pip value per lot)

Work it through with the defaults above: a $5,000 account risking 1% is $50. With a 25-pip stop and $10 pip value, that's $50 ÷ (25 × 10) = 0.20 lots — two mini lots, or twenty micro lots. Widen the stop and the size shrinks; tighten it and the size grows. Your dollar risk stays fixed at $50 either way. That's the whole point.

Why size from risk, not from conviction

New traders size up when they "feel sure." That's exactly backwards. Conviction doesn't change the odds of a single trade — it just makes the loss bigger when you're wrong. Sizing from a fixed risk rule means no single trade can take a meaningful bite out of your account, no matter how confident you were.

If you want the deeper background on the units themselves, read What Is a Lot Size? first. Then pair this with a defined risk-to-reward ratio so every trade has a planned downside and a planned upside.
